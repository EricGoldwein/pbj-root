# PBJ320 interpretation rules: averages, outliers, bad data, and red flags

Use this reference when interpreting PBJ320 charts, tables, exports, or screenshots.

## Core rule

PBJ staffing data is useful because it is structured, facility-level, and time-based. It is dangerous when treated as more precise than it is.

The right posture is: **show the pattern, explain the limits, ask the next better question.**

## Mean vs median

The mean answers: “What is the average across the selected period?”

The median answers: “What did the typical day look like?”

Both can be useful. Neither is automatically superior.

Use the mean when:

- the distribution is fairly stable
- the user needs a quarter-level summary
- comparing to a published benchmark that uses averages
- summarizing total staffing intensity over a period

Use or request the median when:

- daily staffing varies sharply
- there are obvious outliers
- a few extreme days may be moving the average
- the user wants to understand the normal operating pattern

Preferred language:

- “The average is useful, but it may hide day-to-day variation.”
- “The median would better describe the typical day if the distribution is skewed.”
- “A few extreme days could move the mean without representing the usual staffing pattern.”

Avoid:

- “The average proves the facility was staffed/understaffed.”
- “The median is always better.”
- “One low day proves a chronic pattern.”

## Sample size

Before interpreting any staffing pattern, check the denominator.

Ask:

- How many days are included?
- Is this a quarter, month, week, 90-day window, or single day?
- How many facilities are in the comparison group?
- How many residents are in the census denominator?
- How many employee rows or unique workers are included?
- Are missing days excluded or counted as zero?

Small samples should produce cautious language.

Example:

“Because this is based on a short date range, it is better treated as a signal for review than as a stable facility pattern.”

## Outliers

Outliers are leads, not conclusions.

A very low RN day, very high contract day, very low CNA day, or extreme HPRD value may reflect:

- a true staffing event
- holiday/weekend pattern
- census swing
- reporting lag
- coding issue
- missing data
- ownership or management transition
- agency staffing event
- data processing artifact

Preferred language:

- “This date stands out and should be checked against source records.”
- “This may be a real staffing signal, a reporting artifact, or both.”
- “The outlier matters most if it fits a broader pattern.”

## Bad data and data-quality problems

PBJ is facility-reported to CMS. PBJ320 organizes and analyzes public CMS releases; it does not independently audit time clocks or payroll systems.

Potential data-quality warnings:

- impossible values
- missing days
- sudden cliff or spike without explanation
- census near zero or implausibly low
- unusually high HPRD driven by low census
- large mismatch between daily data and quarterly summaries
- employee-detail rows that do not reconcile cleanly with summary hours
- duplicate-looking rows
- role labels that require CMS job-code context

When bad data is possible, say so directly.

Do not force a clean story onto messy data.

## Red flags

PBJ320 red flags are screening signals.

They are useful for prioritizing review. They are not findings of fault.

A red flag may mean:

- low staffing
- unusual role mix
- repeated low days
- volatility
- heavy contract reliance
- mismatch between acuity and staffing
- possible data issue

It does not by itself mean:

- neglect occurred
- a resident was harmed
- a facility broke the law
- staffing caused an event
- CMS verified the concern

## 90-day CNA / aide patterns

A 90-day CNA/aide window is useful because aide staffing often reflects hands-on care capacity and day-to-day stability.

But interpret it carefully.

Ask:

- Is the 90-day signal persistent or driven by a few days?
- Is the average different from the typical day?
- Are low CNA days clustered on weekends, holidays, or specific weeks?
- Is census stable during the window?
- Is contract staffing rising or replacing core staff?
- Do complaints, citations, or survey findings overlap with the window?
- What do assignment sheets, schedules, and payroll records show?

Useful language:

- “The 90-day CNA pattern may raise continuity or hands-on-care capacity questions.”
- “This should be checked against schedules and assignment sheets before drawing resident-level conclusions.”
- “The signal is stronger if repeated low-aide days appear across the window rather than only one or two outliers.”

Avoid:

- “The 90-day CNA number proves neglect.”
- “Low CNA staffing means every resident received poor care.”
- “This establishes causation.”

## Comparison groups

Always ask: compared with what?

Possible comparison groups:

- state facilities
- national facilities
- same owner/operator
- similar size/census facilities
- same facility over time
- before/after ownership or management change
- weekday vs weekend
- incident window vs baseline window

A facility can look bad nationally but typical in its state, or bad versus its own history but average compared with peers. Say which comparison is being used.

## Good PBJ320 analysis sentence pattern

Use this form:

“PBJ320 shows [specific metric/pattern] over [date range/comparison group]. That may suggest [cautious interpretation], especially if confirmed by [source records/context]. It does not by itself prove [unsupported conclusion].”
