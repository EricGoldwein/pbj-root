# PBJ320 AI Support

Use PBJ320 with Claude or ChatGPT — without losing the evidence trail.

PBJ320 helps people understand public nursing home staffing data. This guide shows how to use AI tools responsibly with PBJ320 facility pages, dashboards, screenshots, and exports: summarize what the data shows, ask better questions, and avoid conclusions the data cannot support.

PBJ320 is built for advocates, families, journalists, researchers, and attorneys who need clearer nursing home staffing context.

## Caution line

AI can help summarize and organize PBJ staffing information. It cannot prove what happened on a specific shift, whether a specific resident received care, whether staffing caused an injury, or whether a legal standard was violated.

## How to use AI with PBJ staffing data

AI tools are useful for summarizing, translating, and pressure-testing staffing data. They are weakest when users ask them to make legal, clinical, or causal conclusions from incomplete records.

### Do this

- Paste the PBJ320 page URL, page text, screenshot, dashboard context, or export.
- Ask the AI to separate “shows,” “suggests,” and “cannot prove.”
- Ask for follow-up questions, not final conclusions.
- Include the facility name, CCN, quarter, state, and relevant date range.
- Ask what additional records would be needed.
- Ask whether averages may be hiding outliers or day-to-day variation.
- Ask whether the sample size is large enough for the claim being made.

### Avoid this

- Do not ask AI to “prove neglect” from PBJ data alone.
- Do not treat CMS case-mix hours as a legal staffing minimum.
- Do not assume daily PBJ data shows exact shift coverage.
- Do not ask for resident-specific conclusions without resident-level records.
- Do not use uncited AI summaries as source evidence.
- Do not ignore bad data, missing data, or outlier-driven averages.

## Main copy-paste prompt

Use the PBJ320 Staffing Review Framework to analyze this nursing home staffing material.

When referring to the source, describe it as “the PBJ320 facility page,” “the PBJ320 dashboard,” “the PBJ320 report,” or “the PBJ320 export,” and distinguish PBJ320’s presentation from the underlying CMS Payroll Based Journal data.

Format your response with these sections:

### Bottom line
Open with **PBJ Summary: [facility name]** (not "Plain-English Takeaway"). Give 2–4 cautious sentences plus one compact Markdown table when quarterly numbers are supplied.

### Key staffing signals
Identify the most important signals available in the material, including RN staffing, LPN staffing, CNA/nurse aide staffing, total nurse staffing, staffing mix, case-mix context, percentiles, daily patterns, 90-day CNA/aide patterns, red flags, and trends where available. **Provide one concise data-visual outcome** tied to the strongest *supported* finding (tiny table, ASCII/Mermaid sketch, chart-ready Markdown with exact pasted values)—or briefly say why no meaningful visual is possible—for the completeness rule documented in Claude Skill **`references/pbj320_visual_requirement.md`**.

### What the data shows
List only conclusions directly supported by the PBJ320 material.

### What the data may suggest
Explain cautious interpretations that may warrant further review, such as low RN staffing, weak aide coverage, staffing below case-mix benchmarks, unusual role mix, worsening trends, outlier status compared with peers, possible data-quality issues, or repeated low-staffing days.

### What the data cannot prove
Clearly state what cannot be concluded from PBJ data alone, including what happened on a specific shift, whether a specific resident received care, whether staffing caused an injury, whether reported staffing data is complete or accurate without further records, or whether the facility violated a legal standard.

### Questions to ask next
Generate practical follow-up questions for advocates, families, journalists, researchers, attorneys, or investigators.

### Better records or context to request
List additional information that would help verify or deepen the analysis, such as shift schedules, assignment sheets, timecards, payroll records, census records, agency invoices, resident acuity records, care plans, incident reports, complaints, citations, survey findings, ownership history, state inspection records, or interviews.

### Check the math and the story
Note whether the analysis depends on a mean, median, percentile, sample size, outlier, 90-day window, or potentially bad data. If the pattern is fragile, say so.

Use cautious, evidence-based language. Do not make legal conclusions. Do not assume neglect, misconduct, negligence, causation, or legal violations. Do not invent facts not shown in the material. If something is unclear, say what additional information would be needed.

## For advocates

PBJ320 AI Support is designed to help advocates turn staffing data into sharper questions, not unsupported accusations.

Useful AI tasks:

- summarize a facility’s staffing profile
- identify facilities that may warrant deeper review
- compare staffing signals across facilities
- draft questions for ombudsman programs, regulators, families, journalists, or facility leadership
- identify what records or context are missing
- translate technical staffing terms into plain English
- check whether averages, outliers, or sample-size issues are weakening the story

Avoid:

- treating PBJ data as proof of neglect
- making resident-specific claims without resident records
- using AI-generated summaries without checking the source page
- confusing a red flag with a final conclusion
