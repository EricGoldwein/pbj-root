PBJ Nursing Home Staffing Data, Explained

Plain-English guide · with Phoebe J

Hub page: Use this guide to understand PBJ staffing data and follow links to deeper explainers—without replacing CMS’s official documentation. PBJ320 is a public lookup built from federal CMS data; it is not CMS, Medicare, or a regulator.

What is PBJ?

Payroll-Based Journal (PBJ) is CMS’s federal nursing home staffing reporting system. Facilities submit paid hours by CMS Table 1 job category and work date; CMS publishes public files for Care Compare, research, and oversight. For submission rules and official datasets, see CMS Payroll-Based Journal (PBJ).

What data does CMS publish?

CMS releases PBJ as public use files and open datasets, including:

  • Daily nurse staffing — hours and census by facility and date (CMS PBJ daily nurse staffing (data.cms.gov)).
  • Quarterly public use files — facility-level PBJ aggregates (CMS PBJ staffing data submission; see also PBJ public use file documentation).
  • Provider Information — Care Compare staffing fields and case-mix benchmarks (Nursing Home Provider Information, 4pq5-n9py).
  • Employee detail (where published) — roster-level hours by employee ID (CMS PBJ employee detail).

PBJ320’s free site focuses on quarterly facility metrics; see data sources for the full list.

What is HPRD?

HPRD (hours per resident day) is the standard staffing comparison metric: total paid nursing hours divided by resident days. It answers, on average, how many hours of nursing time were available per resident per day in the reporting period.

Example: 400 nursing hours and 100 resident days → nursing HPRD of 4.0. See What is HPRD? for role-specific measures (RN HPRD, LPN HPRD, nurse aide HPRD) and caveats.

Policy discussions sometimes cite a 4.1 total direct-care HPRD level from a 2001 CMS report to Congress on minimum nurse staffing ratios (hosted on the U.S. Department of Justice elder justice archive). Some states set their own minimums (e.g. 3.5 HPRD in certain statutes—see MACPAC state staffing compendium). These are research or policy references, not values stored as federal legal minimums in PBJ files. Verify current law and enforcement separately.

Key PBJ nursing roles

PBJ groups hours into CMS Table 1 categories—for example RN director of nursing, RN with administrative duties, direct RN, LPN/LVN (including admin), certified nurse aides, and medication aides (nursing codes 5–12 in the PBJ Policy Manual). PBJ also separates employee vs contract hours. Role categories help compare staffing mix; they do not show individual skill, assignment, or care quality.

What PBJ data can show

PBJ data can identify staffing patterns and questions worth investigating—for example:

  • Whether reported nurse HPRD is low or trending down over quarters.
  • How RN, LPN, and aide hours compare within a facility or state.
  • Reliance on contract staff where reported.
  • How a facility compares to state peers on quarterly aggregates (screening only).

Staffing is one of the core inputs used to evaluate nursing home care, but PBJ data should be interpreted alongside inspections, complaints, ownership, resident acuity, and facility context.

What PBJ data cannot prove

PBJ is screening and context data, not proof of harm, neglect, abuse, fraud, or regulatory violations by itself. It does not show:

  • Shift-level bedside assignments or supervision.
  • Staff experience, training, or individual performance.
  • Care outcomes, pain management, or resident satisfaction.
  • Legal compliance with RN coverage rules or state statutes without other records.

Verify important figures on Medicare Care Compare and official CMS files before relying on them in reporting, litigation, or care decisions.

How PBJ320 uses PBJ data

PBJ320 maps published CMS PBJ and related datasets to searchable facility pages, state and national reports, and limited ownership context. The site highlights quarterly HPRD, role mix, case-mix benchmarks from Provider Information, and within-state comparisons where available.
